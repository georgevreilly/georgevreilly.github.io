There's no difference, you say?</p> <p>There <em>is</em> a difference, but it's subtle. The first <tt class="docutils literal">i</tt> in the URL is <a class="reference external" href="http://www.fileformat.info/info/unicode/char/0456/index.htm">'CYRILLIC SMALL LETTER BYELORUSSIAN-UKRAINIAN I' (U+0456)</a>, not <a class="reference external" href="http://www.fileformat.info/info/unicode/char/0069/index.htm">'LATIN SMALL LETTER I' (U+0069)</a>. Depending upon the font, the two <tt class="docutils literal">i</tt>s may be visually indistinguishable, very similar looking, or the Cyrillic <tt class="docutils literal">i</tt> may not render.</p> <p>This is an example of an <a class="reference external" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IDN_homograph_attack">International Domain Name Homograph Attack</a>. There are Greek letters and Cyrillic letters that look very similar to Latin letters, but which have distinct meanings, histories, and code points. International Domain Names permit the construction of non-Latin domain names.</p> <p>Since domain name labels may only contain Latin letters, digits, and hyphens, an encoding scheme known as <a class="reference external" href="https://www.punycoder.com/">Punycode</a> transforms Unicode domain names into ASCII domain names.
